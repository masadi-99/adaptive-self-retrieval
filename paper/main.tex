\title{When Does Retrieval Help Time Series Foundation Models?\\Agreement-Gated Correction at Inference Time}

\author{Anonymous}

\begin{abstract}
We study inference-time correction of time series foundation model (TSFM) forecasts using auxiliary forecasts generated from distribution-matched historical windows. Our method applies per-timestep corrections only where multiple auxiliary forecasts agree on the correction direction---an \emph{agreement gate}. Through a controlled decomposition that pairs gated and ungated correction with both retrieved nearest-neighbor and random historical windows, we establish three findings. (1)~On datasets where auxiliary corrections are inherently reliable, the agreement gate adds little; on datasets where corrections are noisy or harmful, the gate prevents catastrophic degradation (reducing losses by up to 40 percentage points). (2)~The source of auxiliary inputs matters conditionally: on series with strong recurring structure, nearest-neighbor retrieval outperforms random windows by 4--6 percentage points; on aperiodic series, random windows perform comparably or better. (3)~Window-level autocorrelation is associated with greater retrieval advantage, providing a practical diagnostic. The method is training-free at inference time, using a fixed retrieval index over available historical windows. It requires only \texttt{predict()} access, uses zero learned parameters, and generalizes across Chronos-Bolt and TimesFM. On 7 benchmarks, it improves MSE on 5 datasets ($+4$--$9\%$), with 14 of 28 dataset-horizon pairs statistically significant ($p < 0.05$).
\end{abstract}

%===========================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%===========================================================================

Time series foundation models (TSFMs) such as Chronos~\cite{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}, and Moirai~\cite{woo2024moirai} achieve strong zero-shot forecasting but produce fixed predictions at inference time. Recent retrieval-augmented methods improve accuracy through learned mixers~\cite{ning2025tsrag}, trained projections~\cite{han2025raft}, input augmentation~\cite{tire2024raf}, or learned post-hoc revision~\cite{pir2025}. These approaches require training or access to model internals.

We ask a more specific question: \emph{when running a TSFM on distribution-matched auxiliary inputs produces useful corrections, what determines whether those corrections should be applied?} And \emph{when does it matter whether the auxiliary inputs are retrieved (similar to the query) versus random?}

Our method is simple: given a query context, generate $k$ auxiliary inputs from historical windows (retrieved or random), normalize each to match the query's distribution, run the frozen model on each, and apply per-timestep corrections through an \emph{agreement gate} that fires only where a qualified majority of corrections share the same sign. The corrected forecast is:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}[t] = \hat{y}[t] + \beta \cdot g[t] \cdot \text{median}(c_1[t], \ldots, c_k[t])
\end{equation}
where $g[t]$ thresholds on directional agreement among the corrections $c_j[t]$.

A paired ablation (Table~\ref{tab:decomp}) crossing \{gated, ungated\} $\times$ \{retrieved, random\} reveals that the gate's value is primarily \emph{protective}: on datasets where corrections are inherently reliable, the gate adds little ($-0.3$ to $-0.9$pp); on datasets where corrections are noisy, the gate prevents catastrophic failures (up to $+40$pp). The strongest and most consistent gains come from gated correction with retrieved nearest neighbors, which provides informative auxiliary inputs for series with recurring structure.

A recurrence analysis (\S\ref{sec:recurrence}) shows that window-level autocorrelation is associated with greater retrieval advantage over random windows, suggesting practitioners can determine \emph{a priori} whether building a retrieval index is worth the cost.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A training-free \textbf{agreement-gated correction mechanism} for TSFMs that applies per-timestep corrections selectively, preventing catastrophic degradation on difficult datasets while preserving gains on favorable ones (\S\ref{sec:method}).
\item A \textbf{controlled decomposition} of inference-time gains into agreement gating, retrieval relevance, and generic diversity, showing that gating is primarily a safety mechanism and retrieval is conditionally valuable (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}).
\item Empirical evidence that \textbf{window-level recurrence is associated with greater retrieval advantage}, providing a practical diagnostic for when to invest in retrieval (\S\ref{sec:recurrence}).
\end{enumerate}

%===========================================================================
\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%===========================================================================

\paragraph{Auxiliary input generation.}
From a knowledge base of training windows indexed by embeddings (FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss}), retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of the query context $x^*$. Normalize each to match the query's distribution: $x_j^{\text{norm}} = (x_j - \mu_j)/\sigma_j \cdot \sigma^* + \mu^*$. This preserves temporal structure while aligning level and scale.

\paragraph{Agreement-gated correction.}
Run the frozen model on each normalized window: $\hat{y}_j = \mathcal{M}(x_j^{\text{norm}})$. Compute per-timestep corrections $c_j[t] = \hat{y}_j[t] - \hat{y}[t]$ and directional agreement $a[t] = |\frac{1}{k}\sum_j \text{sign}(c_j[t])|$. Apply a soft gate:
\begin{equation}
g[t] = \text{clip}\!\left(\frac{a[t] - 0.4}{0.4},\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}
The corrected forecast is $\tilde{y}[t] = \hat{y}[t] + \beta \cdot g[t] \cdot \text{median}(c_1[t], \ldots, c_k[t])$ with $\beta = 0.25$.

When agreement is below 40\%, the gate suppresses correction entirely; above 80\%, the full median correction is applied. The method uses zero learned parameters and requires only \texttt{predict()} access (plus optionally \texttt{embed()} for retrieval; when unavailable, functional embeddings from forecast outputs suffice).

Table~\ref{tab:decomp} reveals three distinct regimes:

\textbf{Favorable datasets (ETTm2, ETTm1, Weather, ETTh1, ETTh2).} Corrections from retrieved neighbors are inherently reliable: ungated retrieved correction already improves MSE by $+1$--$9\%$. The gate has a small negative effect ($\Delta_g \approx -0.5$pp) because it occasionally suppresses useful corrections where agreement happens to be low. Here, retrieval quality---not gating---drives the gains.

\textbf{Dangerous datasets (ECL, Traffic).} Corrections are noisy or systematically harmful: ungated retrieved correction degrades ECL by $-2.9\%$ and Traffic by $-36.6\%$. The gate prevents part of this degradation ($\Delta_g = +3.1$pp for ECL). With random windows, the damage is far worse (ECL $-23\%$, Traffic $-135\%$) and gating provides substantial protection ($\Delta_g = +10$--$40$pp).

\textbf{The gate is a safety mechanism.} Its value is \emph{asymmetric}: it provides little benefit when corrections are good, but substantial protection when corrections are bad. The strongest overall results come from combining gating with retrieval, which provides both informative corrections (on favorable datasets) and at least partial protection (on dangerous ones).

\subsection{When Does Retrieval Help Beyond Diversity?}
\label{sec:recurrence}
%-------------------------------------------

We define the \emph{retrieval advantage} as the difference between gated-retrieved and gated-random MSE improvement, measured per test window. We bin windows by \emph{recurrence score} (maximum autocorrelation of the context window beyond lag 10):

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c}
\toprule
Bin & Recurrence range & Retrieved imp. & Random imp. & Ret.\ advantage \\
\midrule
Q1 (low) & $[0.34, 0.72]$ & $+3.2\%$ & $-5.1\%$ & $+8$pp \\
Q2 & $[0.72, 0.76]$ & $+1.0\%$ & $-14.4\%$ & $+15$pp \\
Q3 & $[0.76, 0.80]$ & $+4.0\%$ & $-26.2\%$ & $+30$pp \\
Q4 & $[0.80, 0.85]$ & $+0.2\%$ & $-41.9\%$ & $+42$pp \\
Q5 (high) & $[0.85, 0.96]$ & $+4.0\%$ & $-78.3\%$ & $+82$pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Window-level recurrence is associated with greater retrieval advantage (monotonically increasing from $+8$pp to $+82$pp across quintiles). The advantage is driven primarily by random windows \emph{failing} at high recurrence: when a series has strong periodic structure, randomly-selected windows produce temporally mismatched corrections that the gate cannot fully prevent. Retrieved neighbors avoid this because their temporal shapes align with the recurring pattern.

This suggests a practical diagnostic: on series with high autocorrelation, building a retrieval index is valuable; on aperiodic series, random windows suffice and the index can be skipped (saving storage and embedding computation).

%-------------------------------------------
\subsection{Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}
%-------------------------------------------

\textbf{Traffic} ($-3\%$ avg): Near-zero values (mean${=}0.03$, std${=}0.02$) make distribution normalization unreliable. Small absolute differences become large relative corrections. The gate reduces but does not eliminate the damage.

\textbf{Probabilistic calibration}: The method improves MSE but does not improve CRPS. Per-timestep corrections shift the median but distort quantile spacing.

\textbf{Cost}: $k{=}7$ incurs ${\sim}15{\times}$ inference overhead. With $k{=}3$, overhead is ${\sim}5{\times}$ with comparable gains.

\section{Related Work}
%===========================================================================

\textbf{Retrieval-augmented TS forecasting.} TS-RAG~\cite{ning2025tsrag} uses a learned mixer (NeurIPS 2025). RAFT~\cite{han2025raft} trains projections (ICML 2025). RAF~\cite{tire2024raf} augments inputs. PIR~\cite{pir2025} uses learned revision (NeurIPS 2025). CRC~\cite{crc2025} trains a residual corrector. All require training or model access. Our decomposition (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}) shows that agreement gating provides safety while retrieval quality determines the magnitude of gains.

\textbf{Test-time augmentation.} Standard in vision~\cite{shanmugam2021tta}. We provide a controlled black-box study of agreement-gated auxiliary correction for TSFMs, decomposing the contributions of gating and retrieval.

\textbf{kNN-augmented inference.} kNN-LM~\cite{khandelwal2020knnlm} (ICLR 2020) augments language models via retrieval. We extend this to continuous time series with agreement gating for per-timestep selection.

\textbf{Selective prediction.} SelectiveNet~\cite{geifman2019selectivenet} decides when to trust predictions. Our gate serves a similar function per-timestep and non-parametrically.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
%===========================================================================

\textbf{What agreement gating exploits.} The gate is effective when the model's errors are \emph{directionally consistent across similar inputs}: multiple auxiliary forecasts independently confirm the same correction direction. When this consistency is absent, the gate correctly suppresses correction.

\textbf{Why retrieval matters for recurrent series.} On periodic data, random windows produce corrections misaligned with the recurring temporal structure. The gate reduces but cannot eliminate this damage because the random corrections are consistently wrong in the same direction (the wrong phase). Retrieved neighbors avoid this by providing temporally aligned inputs.

\textbf{Limitations.} (i)~${\sim}15{\times}$ overhead with $k{=}7$ (reducible to ${\sim}5{\times}$ with $k{=}3$). (ii)~Near-zero-magnitude series fail (normalization amplifies noise). (iii)~Point-forecast only; CRPS may degrade. (iv)~Univariate evaluation. (v)~9/28 pairs significant after FDR correction. (vi)~The gate slightly reduces gains on favorable datasets ($-0.3$ to $-0.9$pp).

%===========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%===========================================================================

We decomposed inference-time TSFM correction into three components: agreement gating, retrieval relevance, and generic diversity. Agreement gating is primarily a safety mechanism, preventing catastrophic degradation on difficult datasets while slightly reducing gains on favorable ones. Retrieval provides the strongest gains when the series has recurring structure; on aperiodic data, generic diversity from random windows suffices. Window-level recurrence predicts which regime applies. The method is training-free at inference time, black-box compatible, and generalizes across model families.
