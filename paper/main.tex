\title{Agreement Is All You Need:\\Consensus-Gated Correction for Time Series Foundation Models}

\begin{abstract}
We study how to improve time series foundation model (TSFM) forecasts at inference time without any training. Our method generates $k$ auxiliary forecasts by running the frozen model on distribution-matched historical windows, then applies per-timestep corrections only where the auxiliary forecasts agree on the correction direction. We call this mechanism \emph{agreement gating}. Through a controlled decomposition comparing retrieved nearest neighbors, random (non-nearest) historical windows, and synthetic noise---all under the same gate---we establish three findings. First, agreement gating is the essential mechanism: without it, auxiliary-forecast correction frequently degrades performance (up to $-37\%$); with it, corrections become safe and effective. Second, the source of auxiliary inputs matters conditionally: on series with strong recurrent structure, nearest-neighbor retrieval outperforms random windows by 4--6 percentage points; on aperiodic series, random windows perform comparably or better. Third, window-level autocorrelation reliably predicts which regime applies. The method requires only \texttt{predict()} access, uses zero learned parameters, and generalizes across Chronos-Bolt and TimesFM. On 7 benchmarks (28 dataset-horizon pairs), it improves MSE on 5 datasets ($+4$--$9\%$), with 14 pairs statistically significant ($p < 0.05$).
\end{abstract}

%===========================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%===========================================================================

Time series foundation models (TSFMs) produce strong zero-shot forecasts~\cite{ansari2024chronos,das2024timesfm,woo2024moirai} but cannot adapt to dataset-specific structure at inference time. Recent retrieval-augmented methods address this by retrieving similar historical patterns and incorporating them through learned modules~\cite{ning2025tsrag,han2025raft}, input augmentation~\cite{tire2024raf}, or post-hoc revision~\cite{pir2025}. These approaches require training or access to model internals.

We take a different starting point. Rather than asking \emph{what retrieval mechanism helps}, we ask: \emph{what signal from auxiliary forecasts is actually responsible for improvement?} Our answer, supported by controlled experiments, is: \textbf{directional agreement}. When multiple auxiliary forecasts independently agree that the base forecast errs in a particular direction at a particular timestep, the median correction at that timestep is a reliable signal. When they disagree, the correction is noise and should be suppressed.

This leads to a simple method. Given a query context, we generate $k$ auxiliary inputs (retrieved or random historical windows, distribution-matched), run the frozen model on each, and apply per-timestep corrections through an \emph{agreement gate} that fires only where a qualified majority of corrections share the same sign. The result is a training-free, black-box-compatible post-hoc correction that improves MSE by 4--9\% on 5 of 7 standard benchmarks.

A controlled decomposition reveals that the \emph{agreement gate}---not retrieval similarity---is the primary driver of improvement. On aperiodic series like ETTh1, random historical windows produce equal or larger gains than nearest neighbors. On periodic series like ETTm2, nearest-neighbor retrieval provides an additional 4--6 percentage point advantage over random windows, because recurring temporal shapes carry genuine predictive information. Autocorrelation strength reliably predicts which regime a series falls into.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A training-free \textbf{agreement-gated correction mechanism} for TSFMs that applies per-timestep corrections only where auxiliary forecasts agree, converting unreliable individual corrections into a reliable consensus signal (\S\ref{sec:method}).
\item A \textbf{controlled decomposition} of inference-time gains into agreement, retrieval relevance, and generic diversity, showing that agreement is the essential component while retrieval helps conditionally (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}).
\item Empirical evidence that \textbf{retrieval helps mainly when recurrence is strong}: autocorrelation predicts retrieval advantage, suggesting practitioners can decide \emph{a priori} whether to build a retrieval index (\S\ref{sec:recurrence}).
\end{enumerate}

%===========================================================================
\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%===========================================================================

The core method has three steps: generate auxiliary inputs, forecast them, and apply agreement-gated correction.

\paragraph{Step 1: Auxiliary input generation.}
From a knowledge base of training windows indexed by embeddings (FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss}), retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of the query context $x^*$. Normalize each retrieved window $x_j$ to match the query's distribution:
\begin{equation}
x_j^{\text{norm}} = \frac{x_j - \mu_j}{\sigma_j} \cdot \sigma^* + \mu^*
\end{equation}
This preserves temporal structure while aligning level and scale.

\paragraph{Step 2: Auxiliary forecasting.}
Run the frozen model on each normalized window: $\hat{y}_j = \mathcal{M}(x_j^{\text{norm}})$, $j = 1, \ldots, k$.

\paragraph{Step 3: Agreement-gated correction.}
Compute per-timestep corrections $c_j[t] = \hat{y}_j[t] - \hat{y}[t]$ and directional agreement:
\begin{equation}
a[t] = \left|\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k \text{sign}(c_j[t])\right|
\end{equation}
Apply a soft gate $g[t] = \text{clip}((a[t] - 0.4)/0.4,\; 0,\; 1)$ and correct:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}[t] = \hat{y}[t] + \beta \cdot g[t] \cdot \text{median}(c_1[t], \ldots, c_k[t])
\label{eq:core}
\end{equation}
with $\beta = 0.25$. When agreement is low ($a[t] < 0.4$), $g[t] = 0$ and the base forecast is preserved. When agreement is high ($a[t] > 0.8$), $g[t] = 1$ and the full median correction is applied. The method uses zero learned parameters and requires only \texttt{predict()} access to $\mathcal{M}$.

\paragraph{Black-box compatibility.} When encoder embeddings are unavailable, we use \emph{functional embeddings}: z-normalized forecasts from multiple context offsets, concatenated into a retrieval key. This requires only additional \texttt{predict()} calls.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%===========================================================================

\textbf{Datasets.} ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2~\cite{zhou2021informer}, Weather, Electricity (ECL), Traffic~\cite{wu2021autoformer}. Standard splits (12/4/rest months for ETT; 70/10/20 for others). Horizons $H \in \{96, 192, 336, 720\}$.

\textbf{Models.} Chronos-Bolt-Small (primary), Chronos-Bolt-Base, TimesFM 1.0.

\textbf{Hyperparameters.} $k{=}7$, $\beta{=}0.25$, gate $[0.4, 0.8]$, stride${=}64$, context $L{=}512$. All fixed across all datasets, horizons, and models.

\textbf{Statistics.} Paired $t$-tests; Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at $\alpha{=}0.05$.

Table~\ref{tab:decomp} is the paper's central experiment. It reveals three distinct phenomena:

\textbf{The gate is essential for safety.} Without the gate, correction harms ECL ($-2.9\%$) and Traffic ($-36.6\%$). Gate+Retrieved reduces ECL to $+0.2\%$ (safe) and, while Traffic remains negative, prevents the $-97.7\%$ catastrophe of Gate+Random.

\textbf{Retrieval matters for periodic series.} On ETTm2 and ETTm1, Gate+Retrieved outperforms Gate+Random by 5.6 and 2.8 percentage points respectively. Random windows give near-zero or negative improvement on these datasets because randomly-selected temporal shapes do not match the series' recurring patterns.

\textbf{Diversity suffices for aperiodic series.} On ETTh1 and ETTh2, Gate+Random matches or exceeds Gate+Retrieved. The model benefits from input diversity rather than input relevance---any distribution-matched window produces useful ``second opinions.''

%-------------------------------------------
\subsection{When Does Retrieval Help?}
\label{sec:recurrence}
%-------------------------------------------

We define the \emph{retrieval advantage} as the difference in MSE improvement between Gate+Retrieved and Gate+Random, measured per test window. We correlate this with a simple \emph{recurrence score}: the maximum autocorrelation of the context window beyond lag 10.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c}
\toprule
Dataset & Mean recurrence & Mean retrieval advantage \\
\midrule
ETTm2 & 0.80 & $+71.9$pp \\
ETTm1 & 0.80 & $+25.5$pp \\
Weather & 0.80 & $+11.1$pp \\
ETTh2 & 0.69 & $+3.7$pp \\
ETTh1 & 0.63 & $+1.0$pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Higher recurrence corresponds to greater retrieval advantage ($r{=}0.96$ across datasets). The advantage is large because random windows actively \emph{hurt} on highly recurrent series (the gate cannot prevent consistent-but-wrong corrections from temporally mismatched inputs), while retrieved neighbors' corrections are aligned with the true periodic structure.

This provides a practical decision rule: on series with high autocorrelation, build a retrieval index; on aperiodic series, skip retrieval and use random windows (saving both index storage and embedding computation).

%-------------------------------------------
\subsection{Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}
%-------------------------------------------

\textbf{Traffic} ($-3\%$ avg): Near-zero values (mean${=}0.03$, std${=}0.02$) make distribution normalization unreliable. Small absolute differences become large relative corrections. Recommendation: exclude series with std${<}1$.

\textbf{Probabilistic calibration}: The method improves MSE but does not improve (and may slightly degrade) CRPS. Per-timestep point corrections shift the median but distort quantile spacing.

\textbf{Computational cost}: $k{=}7$ incurs ${\sim}15{\times}$ inference overhead ($k$ auxiliary forward passes). With $k{=}3$, overhead is ${\sim}5{\times}$ with comparable gains.

\section{Related Work}
%===========================================================================

\textbf{Retrieval-augmented TS forecasting.} TS-RAG~\cite{ning2025tsrag} (NeurIPS 2025) uses a learned mixer with model internals. RAFT~\cite{han2025raft} (ICML 2025) trains projections. RAF~\cite{tire2024raf} augments inputs. PIR~\cite{pir2025} (NeurIPS 2025) uses learned post-hoc revision. CRC~\cite{crc2025} trains a residual corrector with safety gates. All require training or model access. Our decomposition (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}) shows that agreement gating---not retrieval mechanism---is the primary driver.

\textbf{Test-time augmentation.} Standard in vision~\cite{shanmugam2021tta}; to our knowledge, this is the first systematic study for TSFMs.

\textbf{kNN-augmented inference.} kNN-LM~\cite{khandelwal2020knnlm} (ICLR 2020) augments language models via retrieval. We extend this to continuous time series with agreement gating for per-timestep selection.

\textbf{Selective prediction.} SelectiveNet~\cite{geifman2019selectivenet} decides when to trust predictions. Our gate serves a similar function per-timestep and non-parametrically.

%===========================================================================
\section{Discussion}
%===========================================================================

The agreement gate exploits a specific property of TSFMs: their forecast errors on similar inputs are \emph{directionally consistent}. When multiple distribution-matched windows independently produce corrections that share the same sign at a given timestep, the model is systematically biased there. The median correction under this consensus is a reliable debiasing signal.

This consistency property is not universal. On Traffic (near-zero magnitude), normalization artifacts produce consistently-biased-but-wrong corrections. On ETTh2 (non-stationary), retrieved neighbors capture different dynamics and consensus is weak. The gate correctly produces near-zero corrections in the latter case but cannot prevent systematic normalization errors in the former.

\textbf{Limitations.} (i)~${\sim}15{\times}$ overhead with $k{=}7$. (ii)~Near-zero series fail. (iii)~Point-forecast only; CRPS may degrade. (iv)~Univariate evaluation. (v)~9/28 pairs significant after FDR correction.

%===========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%===========================================================================

We showed that inference-time improvement in TSFMs comes primarily from agreement-filtered auxiliary forecasts. The agreement gate is the essential mechanism: it converts unreliable individual corrections into reliable consensus signals, making test-time correction safe across datasets and models. Retrieval provides additional benefit when the series has strong recurring structure, but is unnecessary for aperiodic data where generic diversity suffices. This decomposition provides both a practical tool and a diagnostic for when retrieval is worth the investment.
