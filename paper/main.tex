\title{Consensus-Gated Test-Time Augmentation\\for Time Series Foundation Models}

\begin{abstract}
Time series foundation models (TSFMs) produce competitive zero-shot forecasts but lack mechanisms for leveraging dataset-specific structure at inference time. We introduce \emph{consensus-gated test-time augmentation}, a training-free method that generates auxiliary forecasts from distribution-matched historical windows and selectively applies per-timestep corrections where a majority of auxiliary forecasts agree on the correction direction. Through controlled ablations comparing five augmentation sources --- retrieved nearest neighbors, random noise, random walks, temporal offsets, and shuffled neighbors --- we find that the \emph{consensus gate} is the critical mechanism, consistently converting unreliable individual corrections into reliable ensemble signals regardless of augmentation source. The method requires only predict() access to the base model, uses zero learned parameters, and generalizes across Chronos-Bolt and TimesFM. On 7 standard benchmarks with corrected evaluation splits, consensus-gated correction improves MSE by 5--9\% on 5 datasets, with 14 of 28 dataset-horizon pairs statistically significant ($p < 0.05$; 9 survive Benjamini-Hochberg correction). We provide honest failure analysis: the method degrades on near-zero-magnitude series (Traffic) and does not improve probabilistic calibration. Code and all results are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

%===========================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%===========================================================================

Time series foundation models (TSFMs) such as Chronos~\cite{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}, and Moirai~\cite{woo2024moirai} achieve strong zero-shot forecasting by pretraining on large corpora. However, at inference time these models produce fixed predictions that cannot adapt to dataset-specific distributional structure. Recent work explores retrieval-augmented approaches: TS-RAG~\cite{ning2025tsrag} uses a learned mixer with model internals, RAFT~\cite{han2025raft} trains retrieval projections, and RAF~\cite{tire2024raf} augments inputs with retrieved contexts. These require training, model access, or both.

We study a simpler question: \emph{can running a frozen model on multiple distribution-matched inputs and selectively combining the forecasts improve accuracy?} This is test-time augmentation (TTA) for time series foundation models --- an approach well-established in computer vision but unexplored for TSFMs.

Our central contribution is the \textbf{consensus gate}: a per-timestep mechanism that applies corrections only where a majority of auxiliary forecasts agree on the correction direction. Without the gate, multi-input ensembling frequently harms performance (up to $-45\%$ on Traffic). With the gate, the same ensembling reliably improves forecasts on 5/7 benchmarks.

A surprising finding emerges from our ablation study: both retrieved neighbors and random noise produce useful auxiliary forecasts under the consensus gate. The gate is the essential mechanism --- it transforms noisy, individually unreliable corrections into a reliable consensus signal. This challenges the implicit assumption in retrieval-augmented forecasting that input similarity is always the key driver.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We introduce consensus-gated test-time augmentation for TSFMs --- a training-free, black-box method that improves forecasts by selectively ensembling auxiliary predictions where directional consensus is strong.
\item We demonstrate through controlled ablation that the consensus gate, not the augmentation source, is the primary driver of improvement, and characterize when retrieval provides additional benefit over random augmentation.
\item We evaluate rigorously across 7 benchmarks, 4 horizons, and 3 foundation models with paired $t$-tests, multiple-comparison correction, cross-model validation, and honest failure analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Overview}

Given a frozen model $\mathcal{M}$ with predict function $\hat{y} = \mathcal{M}(x)$ mapping context $x \in \mathbb{R}^L$ to forecast $\hat{y} \in \mathbb{R}^H$, and a set of historical windows from training data, we:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Generate $k$ auxiliary inputs by retrieving similar windows and normalizing them to match the current context's distribution (mean and standard deviation).
\item Run $\mathcal{M}$ on each auxiliary input to produce $k$ alternative forecasts.
\item Compute per-timestep corrections and apply them through a consensus gate.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Auxiliary Input Generation}

For each query context $x^*$ with statistics $(\mu^*, \sigma^*)$, we retrieve the $k$ nearest windows $\{x_j\}$ from a FAISS index built on encoder embeddings (or functional embeddings for black-box models). Each retrieved window is distribution-normalized:
\begin{equation}
x_j^{\text{norm}} = \frac{x_j - \mu_j}{\sigma_j} \cdot \sigma^* + \mu^*
\end{equation}
producing inputs with the same mean and standard deviation as $x^*$ but different temporal structure.

\subsection{Consensus-Gated Correction}

For each auxiliary forecast $\hat{y}_j = \mathcal{M}(x_j^{\text{norm}})$, compute the per-timestep correction $c_j[t] = \hat{y}_j[t] - \hat{y}[t]$. The directional consensus at timestep $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
a[t] = \left|\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k \text{sign}(c_j[t])\right|
\end{equation}

A soft gate converts consensus to correction weight:
\begin{equation}
g[t] = \text{clip}\!\left(\frac{a[t] - 0.4}{0.4},\; 0,\; 1\right)
\end{equation}

The corrected forecast is:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}[t] = \hat{y}[t] + \beta \cdot s \cdot g[t] \cdot \text{median}(c_1[t], \ldots, c_k[t])
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ controls correction strength and $s$ is an uncertainty scaling factor that reduces corrections on low-uncertainty (easy) windows.

\subsection{Uncertainty Scaling}

The model's prediction interval width $u$ for each window provides a natural difficulty signal:
$s = 0.3 + 0.7 / (1 + \exp(-z_u))$ where $z_u = (u - \bar{u})/\sigma_u$. High-uncertainty windows receive stronger corrections; low-uncertainty windows are left closer to the base forecast.

\section{Experiments}
%===========================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Datasets.} ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2~\cite{zhou2021informer}, Weather, ECL, Traffic~\cite{wu2021autoformer}. Standard 12/4/rest month splits for ETT; 70/10/20 for others. Horizons $H \in \{96, 192, 336, 720\}$.

\textbf{Models.} Chronos-Bolt-Small (primary), Chronos-Bolt-Base, TimesFM 1.0. Same hyperparameters across all models ($k\!=\!7$, $\beta\!=\!0.25$).

\textbf{Hyperparameters.} $k\!=\!7$, $\beta\!=\!0.25$, gate bounds $[0.4, 0.8]$, KB stride 64. All fixed; no per-dataset tuning. Validation study confirms robustness: $k\!\in\!\{3,5,7\}$ and $\beta\!\in\!\{0.2,0.3,0.4\}$ all yield positive results when selected on ETTm2 val.

\textbf{Statistics.} Paired $t$-tests on per-window MSEs. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction at $\alpha\!=\!0.05$.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reveals three findings:

\textbf{(1) The gate works with multiple input sources.} Retrieved neighbors, random noise, and shuffled neighbors all produce improvements under the consensus gate on most datasets.

\textbf{(2) Without gating, correction harms.} In separate ungated experiments (not shown), naive correction degrades Traffic by $-45\%$ and ECL by $-9\%$. The gate converts unreliable corrections into a safe, selective signal.

\textbf{(3) Temporal offsets (sub-windows of the same context) do not help.} The model needs \emph{different} inputs, not resliced versions of the same input. This rules out simple ensembling-by-shifting.

\subsection{Gate Analysis}

Per-window analysis confirms the gate's selectivity. On Weather, high-consensus windows ($a > \text{median}$) improve by $+6.6\%$ while low-consensus windows improve by only $+0.3\%$ (6.3pp gap, $r = 0.29$). This confirms the gate intervenes where evidence is strong and abstains where it is ambiguous.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

\textbf{Traffic} ($-3.0\%$ avg, significant at $H\!=\!96$): Near-zero magnitude (mean$=0.03$, std$=0.02$) makes distribution normalization unreliable --- small absolute errors become large relative corrections.

\textbf{ETTh2} ($+0.3\%$, not significant): High non-stationarity. Retrieved neighbors capture different dynamics despite embedding similarity. The gate correctly produces near-zero corrections.

\textbf{Probabilistic calibration}: The method improves point forecasts (MSE) but slightly degrades quantile calibration (CRPS), since corrections shift the median toward ground truth but distort quantile spacing. This is a fundamental limitation of per-timestep point corrections.

\subsection{Computational Cost}

With $k\!=\!7$, inference cost is $\sim$15$\times$ baseline (dominated by $k$ auxiliary forward passes). With $k\!=\!3$, cost reduces to $\sim$5$\times$ with comparable gains. No training cost. Auxiliary passes are embarrassingly parallelizable.

\section{Related Work}
%===========================================================================

\textbf{Retrieval-augmented forecasting.} TS-RAG~\cite{ning2025tsrag} learns an Adaptive Retrieval Mixer for Chronos (NeurIPS 2025). RAFT~\cite{han2025raft} trains retrieval projections (ICML 2025). RAF~\cite{tire2024raf} concatenates retrieved contexts as input prefixes. PIR~\cite{pir2025} uses learned post-hoc revision. CRC~\cite{crc2025} trains a residual corrector with safety gates. Our method differs: it is training-free, black-box, and the ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) shows the consensus gate --- not retrieval quality --- is the primary mechanism.

\textbf{Test-time augmentation.} TTA is well-established in computer vision~\cite{shanmugam2021tta} and has been applied to tabular data. To our knowledge, this is the first systematic study of TTA for time series foundation models.

\textbf{kNN-augmented inference.} kNN-LM~\cite{khandelwal2020knnlm} demonstrated training-free retrieval augmentation for language models. We extend this paradigm to continuous time series, where the key challenge --- addressed by consensus gating --- is determining which timesteps benefit from correction.

\textbf{Selective prediction.} SelectiveNet~\cite{geifman2019selectivenet} and learn-to-defer frameworks~\cite{mozannar2020lfd} decide when to trust a model vs.\ abstain. Our consensus gate serves a similar function but is non-parametric and applied per-timestep rather than per-instance.

%===========================================================================
\section{Limitations}
%===========================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Traffic-type failures.} Near-zero-magnitude series cause distribution normalization to amplify noise. A magnitude check (std $> 1$) prevents deployment.
\item \textbf{Point forecasts only.} MSE improves but CRPS may degrade. Per-timestep corrections shift quantiles rather than calibrating them.
\item \textbf{Computational overhead.} $k\!=\!7$ incurs 15$\times$ cost (5$\times$ with $k\!=\!3$). Acceptable for batch forecasting but not real-time.
\item \textbf{Univariate evaluation.} Multivariate extension is untested.
\item \textbf{Statistical power.} Long-horizon and small-dataset settings ($N < 10$) lack power for significance testing.
\end{itemize}

%===========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%===========================================================================

We introduced consensus-gated test-time augmentation for time series foundation models. The key finding is that the \emph{consensus gate} --- which applies corrections only where multiple auxiliary forecasts agree --- is the essential mechanism that makes test-time correction reliable. The gate works with retrieved neighbors, random noise, and shuffled inputs, suggesting that TSFMs benefit from structured disagreement at inference time regardless of how auxiliary inputs are generated. The method is training-free, black-box compatible, and generalizes across model families.
